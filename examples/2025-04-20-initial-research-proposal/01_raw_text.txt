(1) Background

Presentations play a crucial role in academic communication, allowing researchers to effectively convey ideas,
findings, and insights to diverse audiences. Among various forms, slide-based presentations are especially
common due to their clarity, portability, and adaptability in both formal conferences and classroom settings.
However, preparing slides—especially for technical content—can be labor-intensive and time-consuming. While
tools like PowerPoint offer design flexibility, they often lack support for logical structuring and formatting.
With the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs), there is growing potential for automating various parts of
academic workflows. LLMs have demonstrated strong capabilities in summarization, content organization, and
knowledge extraction. This opens up opportunities to develop systems that generate presentation materials from
academic documents such as research papers, reports, or theses. Particularly, these models can be adapted to
produce slides in textual formats using tools such as LaTeX Beamer, commonly used in scientific fields.
(2) Purpose, Goals, and Justification
This research aims to design an automated system that transforms structured academic documents into
presentation slides using fine-tuned LLMs. The objectives are:
•To streamline the creation of logically structured, academically formatted slides;
•To support audience-specific customization (e.g., technical vs. general);
•To provide speaker notes that assist in oral delivery;

•To enhance the efficiency and consistency of academic communication.

This approach addresses a gap in existing document-to-slide (D2S) tools, which rarely consider both content
precision and academic presentation norms.
(3) Methods
• Data Collection: Gather high-quality slides from leading academic conferences and open course materials.
Reconstruct them in a standardized, structured format (e.g., LaTeX Beamer) to create a parallel dataset aligned
with original source documents.
•Model Fine-tuning: Fine-tune a pre-trained LLM to identify and extract key elements (e.g., section titles, bullet
points, formulas, figures) from documents and convert them into slide-format text. Use parameter-efficient
tuning methods to reduce resource demands.
• Audience Adaptation: Train the system to generate different versions of slides based on audience type and
presentation length.

•Evaluation: Assess the system output using automatic metrics (e.g., ROUGE) and human evaluations (e.g., clarity,
coherence, academic tone).
(4) Expected Results
The outcome will be a novel tool that automates high-quality academic slide generation. Its contribution lies in
combining LLM-based summarization with structured presentation generation in a way that accommodates
academic standards and varying audience needs. Compared to existing solutions, the system is expected to
produce more coherent, readable, and purpose-adapted presentations—benefiting students, researchers, and
educators alike.
